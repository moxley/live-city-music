G H I J K L M

G[edit]

Ga - Ge-Gn - Go-Gr - Gu-Gy

  • G-funk - style of West Coast gangsta rap; known for heavy funk influence

Gaa-Gal[edit]

  • Gaana - upbeat Tamil dance song performed at celebrations; and frequently used as filmi in Tamil cinema
  • Gabber - a faster, more anarchistic, form of house music designed to counter the pretentious Dutch house scene of the 1980s; evolved into hardcore techno
  • Gagaku - any Japanese classical music played for the Imperial Court; often either kuniburi no utami (Shinto religious music), komagaku (Goguryeo and Manchurian form from the Three Kingdoms period), or togaku (form from the Tang Dynasty).
  • Gaita Zuliana - diverse form of Venezuelan folk
  • Galant - intentionally simplistic style of Western classical music designed to counter the increasingly complex Baroque music of the 18th century

Gam-Gan[edit]

  • Gamelan - Indonesian classical music
  • Gamelan bebonangan - Balinese style of gamelan that utilizes a 7-tone scale and cymbals
  • Gamelan degung - Sudanese style of gamelan that uses the pegog scale
  • Gamelan gong kebyar - Balinese style of gamelan known for its explosive changes in tempo
  • Gamelan salendro - West Javan gamelan; usually played at puppet shows (or wayang golek)
  • Gamelan selunding - Balinese style of gamelan; generally considered the oldest form
  • Gamelan semar pegulingan - Balinese style of gamelan; known for sweet and sensual melodies
  • Gammaldans - wide variety of traditional Nordic dance music, and modernized versions created by Nordic-Americans
  • Gandrung - traditional Indonesian dance music
  • Gangsta rap - hip hop that deals with illegal activity; usually used to refer to music based around life in poverty, rather than later forms of 'glamour rap'

Gar-Gav[edit]

  • Gar - Tibetan chant music
  • Garage house - heavily polished style of American house
  • Garage rock - raw and energetic style of rock, often practised by high school bands in garages; considered a precursor to punk
  • Gothic hymn - gothic styled hymns
  • Gavotte - traditional French dance music

Ge-Gn[edit]

  • Gender wayang - Balinese style of gamelan; used to accompany Hindu ceremonies
  • German folk - any folk music performed by Germans
  • Gharnati - Algerian folk music
  • Ghazal - Arabic (particularly Pakistani) angst-ridden poetry, often accompanied by music
  • Ghetto house - form of Chicago house known for its sexually explicit lyrics; often influenced by hip hop
  • Ghettotech - fusion of Chicago house, Miami bass, electro, glitch, and techno; known for sexually explicit lyrics and gritty beats
  • Girl group - any all-female pop or rock group
  • Glam metal - fusion of glam and hard rock; often considered the pop-rock standard of the 1980s
  • Glam punk - fusion of glam and punk rock; which existed long before mainstream punk
  • Glam rock - loosely-defined art rock which included heavy themes of gender-bending and androgyny
  • Glitch - style of EDM based around samples of malfunctioning technology in order to create an intentionally harsh sound; perhaps as a way to illustrate the downside of the transhumanist utopia implied by techno and electro
  • Gwana - Islamic African religious music

Go-Gr[edit]

  • Go-go - style of funk known for its syncopated rhythms and call-and-response vocals
  • Goa trance - fusion of trance music and traditional Indian styles
  • Gong chime - any music performed with high-pitched pot gongs, usually Southeast Asian styles
  • Goombay - Bahamian drum music
  • Goregrind - style of grindcore known for its lyrical focus on gore and forensics
  • Goshu ondo - traditional Japanese dance music from the Meiji era
  • Gospel - an attempt to modernize religious music; gospel was a style of religious song inspired by country; particularly popular in African-American communities
  • Gothic metal - fusion of gothic rock and heavy metal; usually based on doom metal
  • Gothic rock - style of post-punk, heavily inspired by Gothic art; usually with a focus on darkness and pain
  • Grebo - a short-lived British style of garage rock from the 1990s
  • Gregorian chant - a capella, monotonic religious chant used by the Roman Catholic Church
  • Grime - fusion of hip hop and UK garage
  • Grindcore - fusion of death metal and hardcore punk
  • Groove metal - style of heavy metal that took elements of thrash, but played at mid-tempo, making a slower, groovier sound
  • Group Sounds - Japanese pop from the 1960s, inspired heavily by British beat and American bubblegum pop; the term is also used to include the psychedelic and folk rock bands of the time and place
  • Grunge - minimalist style of alternative metal, known for its heavily distorted guitars and angst-ridden lyrics
  • Grupera - rock-inspired Mexican folk

Gu-Gy[edit]

  • Guajira - Cuban country music, performed in rural communities
  • Gumbe - Guinea-Bissaun folk music
  • Gunchei - Central American music played to accompany the garifauna dance of the same name
  • Gunka - Japanese military music
  • Guoyue - modernized Chinese traditional music
  • Gwo ka - Guadaloupean drum music
  • Gwo ka moderne - modernized form of gwo ka
  • Gypsy jazz - Roma-French style of jazz
  • Gypsy punk - Romani style of punk rock

H[edit]

Ha - He-Ho - Hu-Hy

Hab-Has[edit]

  • Habanera - African-American style based on Cuban contredanza
  • Halling - Norwegian and Swedish folk music made to accompany the dance of the same name
  • Hambo - Swedish folk music made to accompany the dance of the same name
  • Hamburger Schule - style of alternative rock based in Hamburg, Germany; informed by pop, punk, and grunge; known for intellectual lyrics
  • Happy hardcore - incredibly fast, upbeat, and optimistic style of hardcore techno; departing from the pessimism of other hardcore styles
  • Haqibah - Sudanese a capella music
  • Hardcore hip hop - aggressive and confrontational form of hip hop; lyrics often pertain to partying, braggadocio, crime, violence, sex, nudity, wrath, poverty, menacing, omen, rebellion, profanity, racism, drugs, weapons, resentment, ghettos, gangs, social issues, consciousness, struggling, nihilism, distrusting, life, death, police brutality, and the harsh experiences of the rapper's urban surroundings
  • Hardcore punk - heavy metal-informed style of punk; unlike earlier acts, hardcore bands were known for their technical proficiency and emphasis on skill; considered a renaissance in a time when punk was losing popularity
  • Hardcore techno - style of techno known for distorted, industrial-esque beats; often very aggressive and pessimistic
  • Hard bop - style of bebop informed by gospel, R&B and blues
  • Hard house - fusion of hardstyle and house music
  • Hard rock - loud, aggressive, distorted, and technically proficient form of rock
  • Hardstep - gritty, heavy style of drum & bass; popular among the junglist subculture
  • Hardstyle - intense, heavy style of EDM known for its heavy kick-drums and reversed basslines
  • Hard trance - heavy, reverberating style of trance music
  • Harmonica blues - blues music that utilizes the Richter-tuned harmonica
  • Hasapiko - Greek folk dance music, originating in Constantinople

Hat-Haz[edit]

  • Hát tuồng (Hát bôi) - Vietnamese opera

He-Ho[edit]

  • Heartland rock - style of rock known for its minimalism, straightforwardness, and concern with the American working class
  • Heavy metal - technically proficient, fast-paced, aggressive form of hard rock
  • Hi-NRG - uptempo, fast-paced style of EDM known for a reverberating, four-on-the-floor rhythm
  • Highlife - Ghanan style that married traditional African forms with Western pop
  • Hiplife - fusion of highlife and hip hop
  • Hip hop - combination of innovative DJ techniques, particularly sampling of pre-recorded material; and the lyrical style of rapping, a rhythmic technique somewhere between singing and spoken word; also known for developing beatboxing, a kind of vocal instrumental; hip hop music was part of a larger culture including breakdancing and graffiti; generally performed by African-Americans, particularly those of the working class
  • Hip house - fusion of hip hop and house music
  • Hindustani classical - Northern Indian classical music
  • Hindustani electronic dhamaka - Music played by India's most popular electronic duo Midival Punditz*
  • Hiragasy - style of music and dance performed by troupes of relatives for day-long periods by the Merina people of Madagascar
  • Honky-tonk - crisp, clean form of country; performed mostly at drinking establishments of the same name
  • Honkyoku - religious music performed by Japanese Zen Buddhists
  • Hora lungă - improvisational Romani folk music
  • Hornpipe - music played to accompany the British naval dance of the same name
  • Horrorcore - hip hop known for dark, horror-inspired lyrics
  • Horror punk - punk that is lyrically inspired by 1950s horror B-movies, often in an ironic way
  • House - a relaxed, disco-informed style of EDM; also the most popular and commercially viable

Hu-Hy[edit]

  • Huayño - Peruvian folk music
  • Hula - Hawaiian folk music made to accompany the dance of the same name
  • Humppa - Finnish jazz style
  • Hunguhungu - folk music performed by Garifuna women
  • Hyangak - Korean court music from the Three Kingdoms period
  • Hymn - any religious song
  • Hyphy - fast-paced style of hip hop from the San Francisco Bay Area

I[edit]

  • Icaro - music sung in healing ceremonies of the Shipibo-Conibo people of Peru
  • Igbo - any music performed by the Igbo people of Nigeria; particularly highlife, odumodu, and waka
  • Illbient - form of ambient inspired by dub in its use of layering and hip hop in its use of sampling
  • Impressionist - style of Western art music inspired by the visual arts movement of the same name; based around an idea of symbolism and minimalism
  • Improvisational - any kind of music that is made up on the spot
  • Incidental - music played in the background of a film or play
  • Indietronica - fusion of indie rock and EDM
  • Indie folk - fusion of indie rock and folk music
  • Indie - music that is formed around an idea of remaining on the underground and a DIY ethic; often considered a watered-down follow-up of punk
  • Indie pop - a melodic, often angst-free and optimistic, form of pop-rock associated with the indie scene; it is generally considered the staple of the modern indie and hipster subcultures
  • Indie rock - generic term for rock music linked to the indie subculture; usually in reference to post-rock, noise, etc.; indie is often confused for the post-punk revivalist scene and the similar indie pop scene
  • Indo jazz - fusion of jazz and traditional Indian music
  • Industrial death metal - fusion of industrial and death metal
  • Industrial hip hop - fusion of industrial and hip hop music
  • Industrial - early form of electronica that linked avant-garde electronic experimentation to punk rock energy, vocalisation, and ethics. Industrial spawned an indulgence in darkness, horror, and even fascism (although often not seriously) that carried over into goth, emo, and metalhead culture
  • Industrial musical - musical theater performed by the workers of a company to promote teamwork
  • Industrial metal - fusion of industrial and heavy metal music
  • Industrial rock - fusion of industrial and rock music; often considered the leading subgenre in post-industrial music
  • Instrumental rock - any rock music that neglects vocals
  • Intelligent dance - more experimental and intellectual form of electronica so called to distinguish itself from the commercialist trends in rave music; the term has been criticized by a number of musicians, including those considered to be IDM
  • Inuit - any music performed by the Inuit people of Greenland and Canada
  • Irish folk - traditional music of the Irish people; usuaully performed using whistles, harps, and fiddles
  • Irish rebel - Irish folk with an emphasis on Irish republicanism
  • Isicathamiya - a capella form of singing used by the Zulu people of South Africa
  • Isolationist - style of ambient that uses repetition and dissonance to create a sense of uneasiness
  • Italo dance - an optimistic form of Eurodance that developed in Italy
  • Italo disco - form of disco developed in Italy that lead to the creation of modern EDM
  • Italo house - Italian house music that followed on from Italo disco
  • Izvorna bosanska - Bosnian rural roots music

J[edit]

Ja-Je - Ji-Ju

  • J-Pop - pop music made by the Japanese; often electropop
  • J-Rock - rock music made by Japanese performers

Ja-Je[edit]

  • Jaipongan - music made to accompany the dance of the same name of Sundanese people of Indonesia
  • Jam - a type of band that plays long instrumental tracks, often improvised, called 'jams'
  • Jamrieng samai - Cambodian pop music
  • Jangle pop - style of indie pop known for its uplifting, 'jangly' sounds
  • Jarana yucateca - traditional Yucatán dance music
  • Jarocho - Mexican dance and song style from Veracruz
  • Jawaiian - fusion of Hawaiian traditional music and reggae
  • Jazz - music played with brass instruments; with an emphasis on swing, improvisation, individuality, and group participation, developed in African-American communities
  • Jazz blues - fusion of jazz and blues music
  • Jazz-funk - fusion of jazz and funk music
  • Jazz fusion - any music that fuses something with jazz; but usually used to refer to jazz-rock
  • Jazz rap - fusion of jazz and hip hop
  • Jegog - gamelan played with bamboo-based instruments
  • Jenkka - Finnish folk dance music
  • Jesus - style of CCM developed by the American hippie-based Jesus Movement

Ji-Jt[edit]

  • Jig - uptempo Irish folk dance music
  • Jing ping - Dominican folk dance music developed by slave during European colonialism
  • Jingle - short, catchy song used in advertising
  • Jit - Zimbabwean pop music
  • Jitterbug - any music that accompanied the dance of the same name; usually swing or rock n roll
  • Jive - swing music used to accompany the African-American ballroom dance of the same name
  • Joged - Balinese dance music; also used as a generic term for any Indonesian dance music
  • Joged bumbung - fusion of gamelan and joged
  • Joik - style of Sami folk music; also used as a generic term for any Sami folk music
  • Joropo - Venezuelan waltz
  • Jota - Spanish folk dance music
  • Jug - African-American folk music made from household objects such as jugs, spoons, and washboards
  • Juke joint blues - fusion of blues and soul
  • Jùjú - Nigerian pop music
  • Jump blues - uptempo blues music played with horns; considered a precursor to R&B
  • Jump-up - faster form of drum & bass
  • Jumpstyle - faster form of progressive house
  • Jungle - style of EDM known for fast tempo, breakbeats, samples, and dub-inspired layered synths
  • Junkanoo - Bahamas folk dance music

K[edit]

Ka - Ke-Kh - Ki-Kp - Kr-Kw

K-[edit]

  • K-pop - Korean pop music

Ka[edit]

  • Kaba - Southern Albanian instrumental folk music
  • Kabuki - form of Japanese musical theatre known for its elaborate make-up and costuming
  • Kagok - Korean folk music
  • Kaiso - Trinidadian folk music; of which calypso is a derivative
  • Kalamatianó - Greek folk music
  • Kan ha diskan - Breton folk music
  • Kansas City blues - blues music performed by Kansas City inhabitants
  • Kantrum - fast-paced Khmer-Thai folk music
  • Kargyraa - deep, growling form of Tuvan throat singing; inspired by Tibetan Buddhist chants
  • Kaseko - Surinamean music that fuses African, European, and American styles
  • Kachāshī - fast-paced Ryukyuan festive folk music
  • Kawachi ondo - Japanese folk music from the Osaka region
  • Kayōkyoku - Japanese pop music; informed by American crooners and rock and roll; early form of J-Pop

Ke-Kh[edit]

  • Kecak - Balinese folk opera; performed by troupes of 150; based on a battle scene from the Hindu epic Ramayana
  • Kacapi suling - Sundanese folk music
  • Kertok - Malay musical ensemble utilizing xylophones
  • Khaleeji - Arab folk music; based on the oub
  • Khene - Malay woodwind music
  • Khyal - North Indian form of Hindustani classical music
  • Khoomei - soft, droning form of Tuvan throat singing

Ki-Kp[edit]

  • Kirtan - Indian drum music performed during Hindu bhakti rituals
  • Kiwi rock - rock music performed by New Zealanders
  • Kizomba - Angolan folk dance music
  • Klapa - Croatian a capella music
  • Klasik - Afghan classical music
  • Klezmer - Jewish classical music
  • Kliningan - Sundanese folk dance music
  • Kolomyjka - tongue-in-cheek Hutsul folk dance music
  • Komagaku - Japanese court music from the Heian period
  • Kpanlogo - Ghanan folk dance music; developed by the Ga people

Kr-Kw[edit]

  • Krakowiak - fast-paced Polish folk dance music
  • Krautrock - highly experimental form of German art rock that incorporated electronic influences; providing the beginning of electro and EDM in general
  • Kriti - Indian classical music; similar to Carnatic music
  • Kroncong - Indonesian folk music utilizing the ukele; heavily informed by Portuguese styles
  • Kuduro - Angolan folk music
  • Kulintang - ancient gong music of the Filipinos, Indonesians, Malays, Bruneian, and Timorese
  • Kundiman - Filipino love songs
  • Kvæði - Icelandic folk music
  • Kwaito - South African house music; informed by a variety of African styles
  • Kwassa kwassa - Congolese folk dance music
  • Kwela - South African skiffle music; often played with the pennywhistle

L[edit]

La - Le-Lo - Lu

La[edit]

  • Lambada - Brazilian dance music
  • Latin pop - fusion of pop music and traditional South American styles
  • Lavani - style of traditional Indian music performed in Maharashtra; often by women with extremely long saris

Le-Lo[edit]

  • Legényes - Hungarian and Romanian folk dance music performed by the inhabitants of Transylvania, now modern-day Cluj-Napoca
  • Letkajenkka - Finnish folk dance music
  • Lhamo - Tibetan folk opera
  • Lied - German poems spoken to music
  • Light - soft, non-confrontational British orchestral music; linked heavily to the easy-listening genre
  • Liquid funk - form of drum and bass with a heavy emphasis on melody; utilizes layered instrumentals inspired by funk and jazz
  • Liquindi - style of percussion performed by the various 'pygmy' peoples of Africa in which drummers stand in a body of water and hit the surface
  • Lo-fi - any music recorded at a quality lower than usual; many unsigned bands use lo-fi recording because it costs less; thus many more popular indie bands purposefully use lo-fi production to have an indie sound
  • Logobi - form of zouglou influenced by the French colonists in the Ivory Coast; later became popular in France and Europe
  • Long song - Mongolian folk music in which each syllable is extended for a longer than average period of time
  • Louisiana blues - any blues performed by inhabitants of the state of Louisiana; generally divided into the jazz-informed New Orleans blues and zydeco-informed swamp blues
  • Lounge - downtempo music intended to give the listener a sense of being somewhere else, i.e. a jungle or outer space; heavily influenced by retro space-age culture
  • Lovers rock - form of reggae fusion known for its romantic lyrics
  • Lowercase - extreme form of ambient music consisting of long periods of silence and occasional, very minute sounds

Lu[edit]

  • Lu - Tibetan a capella music
  • Lubbock sound - fusion of rock and roll and country music from Lubbock, Texas; popularized by Buddy Holly; lead to the development of rockabilly and greaser music; and thus rock music in general; Holly was the first to use the now standard line-up of two guitars, bass, drums, and vocals
  • Luk Krung - more polished form of luk thung
  • Luk thung - Thai folk music; most popular style of Thai music
  • Lullaby - soothing song sung to young children to lull them to sleep
  • Lundu - harmonious style of Afro-Brazilian music

M[edit]

Ma - Mb-Mg - Mi - Min-Mir - Mo-Mp - Mu

  • M-Base - style of musical thought and composition developed by Steve Coleman; M-Base music should be improvised, relevant, expressive, philosophical, and inclusive of non-Western styles; many musicians associated with the M-Base concept fall under the category of jazz

Mad-Mam[edit]

  • Madchester - fusion of EDM, psychedelic rock, and indie rock
  • Madrigal - style of classical singing popular in the Renaissance and Baroque eras
  • Mafioso rap - subgenre of gangsta rap that focuses on organized crime
  • Mahori - form of Thai and Khmer classical music
  • Makossa - Cameroonian pop; informed by traditional Congolese styles, as well as jazz, highlife, and Latin styles
  • Malhun - Arab folk poetry; usually found in the Maghreb region
  • Maloya - style of folk developed by the slaves on the French territory of Reunion; often compared to blues music
  • Mambo - Cuban style of jazz

Man-Map[edit]

  • Manaschi - Kyrgyz song recital of the Epic of Manas
  • Mandopop - style of C-pop sung in the Mandarin language
  • Manele - Romani folk music
  • Mangue Bit - Brazilian electronic genre played in a fast-paced, punk-informed style; lyrics usually concern class issues; played almost exclusively in the northeast city of Recife
  • Manila Sound - fusion of Western rock music and traditional Filipino folk music; played almost exclusively in the capital city of Manila; considered strangely optimistic for the time of political turmoil it was played during
  • Mapouka - traditional folk dance music of the Aizi, Alladian, and Avikam people of the Ivory Coast; the dance of the same name is considered a precedent to the American twerking

Mar-Maz[edit]

  • Marabi - South African style informed by blues and jazz
  • Maracatu - Brazilian folk dance music
  • Mariachi - fusion of Mexican folk music and pop music
  • Marrabenta - Mozambican folk dance music informed by Portuguese styles
  • Martial industrial - style of neo-folk informed by military marches and militaristic themes; use of fascist imagery and themes has lead to the martial-industrial subculture to be branded a far-right group by organisations such as the Anti-Defamation League, although many military-industrial groups are left-wing or apolitical
  • Maskanda - South African folk music; maskanda began with Zulus but expanded to other cultures living in the country
  • Marinera - romantic Peruvian folk dance music
  • Martinetes - a capella flamenco music
  • Mass - Christian hymns sung by large vocal groups; often sung a capella
  • Matamuerte - Garifuna folk dance music
  • Mathcore - fusion of metalcore and math rock; often linked to the grindcore scene
  • Math rock - rhythmically complex form of experimental rock; often considered a modern-day expression of progressive rock
  • Maxixe - Brazilian folk dance music
  • Mazurka - Polish folk dance music

  • Mbaqanga (township jive)

  • Mor lam sing - popular form of Laotian traditional music developed by Laotians in Thailand
  • Moombahton

  • Melbourne house
  • Mugam - classical music of Azerbaijan
  • Multicultural
  • Murga - Uruguayan street carnival dance with heavy percussion, also popular in Argentina.
  • Musette
  • Mushroom Jazz
  • Music drama
  • Music Hall
  • Música criolla - a coastal Peruvian music from the early 20th century, consisting of a variety of Western fusions
